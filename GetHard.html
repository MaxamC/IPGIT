Get Hard

A movie with a motive.
“Get Hard” (directed by Etan Cohen) is mostly Will Ferrell and Kevin Hart doing what they do best - making people laugh. Underneath that exterior though, is a movie that tries to take a stab at addressing the economic disparity that has come to all but define the nation. At no point is this done better than in the opening credits, and while that may sound like a slight, I’m being sincere. As the opening credits roll, they are accompanied by slices of life in the days of 1-Percenters vs the 99-Percenters. Contrasting shots of people flooding into a fancy office building and flooding to get on a bus, or a particularly pointed shot of a foreman pointing to workers that he wants to use juxtaposed to a woman pointing at oranges she wants from a farmer’s market. After this, the film mostly glosses over the hard hitting issues (and a good thing too - if I bought tickets to a Will Ferrell movie and end up in an Econ 101 class, I will have some choice words for AMC). Still, it starts the conversation, giving the audience a chance to think more about the issue if they want.

For the continuity connoisseur, there’s one scene in the film that raises red flags. In a scene set in Ferrell’s backyard tennis court of his Bel Air mansion, Kevin Hart does a brilliant job at playing three different “jail yard” characters at the same time. However, if you look behind Hart, you’ll see a city street with parked cars and driveways. Maybe the filmmakers saw this and wanted the audience to assume that Ferrell’s character was so wealthy that his backyard contained an entire middle class neighborhood, but more than likely the production team couldn’t find a willing mansion owner who wanted to turn his backyard tennis court into a prison yard. This is far from the first time that the makers of “movie magic” have tried to make one place look like another, but this one just happens to be laughable. Keep your eyes peeled for a peak behind the Hollywood curtain, and have a good time!
