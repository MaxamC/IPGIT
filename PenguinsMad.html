Penguins of Madagascar

You get exactly what you pay for with “Penguins of Madagascar” - cute penguins being commandos and saying funny things. Co-directors Eric Darnell and Simon J. Smith keep this Thanksgiving family comedy right where it needs to be, cartoony and fun, and the result is a very watchable movie that really is fun for the whole family. This isn’t the same type of feature as “Wall E” or “Tangled”, where an animated movie told a real story and created real drama. “Penguins” is a small, silly film that sets out to simply entertain and it more than hits it’s mark - it gets four tiny penguins to demolish it.


Genre

“Penguins of Madagascar” is less an animated film, and more a cartoon. Its plot is obvious and safe and it doesn’t matter. It’s awesome in its silliness and cuteness and simplicity. It has the same dramatic tension as a Bugs Bunny cartoon, but isn't that exactly why I bought the ticket? I paid $14 in exchange for two hours of mindless entertainment - and “Penguins” delivers.

Performance

None of the heavies from the “Madagascar” series come back to play with their polar pals, but this leaves room for John Malkovich to give his voice to a huge octopus. I know that a lot of you think I just described the plot of “The Little Mermaid”, but I did not. John Malkovich plays the voice of an evil, purple, bucktoothed (octopi have mouths where we do in cartoon-land) octopus in “Penguins” and it’s amazing. Malkovich has played so many memorable characters and mastered so many voices (who can forget Teddy KGB in “Rounders”?) and he slips easily into the world of animation. The voice acting from the four penguins is so strong that it would be easy to be forgettable (Benedict Cumberbatch proves this with his performance as a wolf) but Malkovich rises to the occasion and more - he’s just plain funny.

Filmmaking

“Penguins of Madagascar” doesn’t bite off more than it can chew - granted it barely nibbles, but just like the TV show that is so successful, “Penguins” doesn’t try to be more than it is. The fact that no one aspect stood out against the others is a testament to how well the film worked as a whole, and points to why it was such a success. Eric Darnell and Simon J. Smith offer little more than a drawn-out episode of the cartoon, and by staying in that sweet spot the movie flourishes. I can barely remember the plot, but it doesn’t matter because I went to see four tiny penguins cause mischief and be adorable and say funny things. They did, they were, and they do.

What To Watch For

Not too many hidden bits of treasure in this one, but definitely keep your ears open for names of celebrities being mixed into the dialogue (e.g. “Helen! Hunt them down!”). This little wink at the parents in the audience is exactly the type of humor that makes “Penguins of Madagascar” such a home run..
