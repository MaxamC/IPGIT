Nightcrawler

I was a little late to the “Nightcrawler” party. Almost a month late, in fact (the Dan Gilroy thriller was released on Halloween). But I wasn’t a film critic back then (how’s THAT for instilling confidence in my readers?), so I had to watch it now. Late or not, the film was a tight story centered around the most interesting performance I’ve seen this year. It may not be exactly what you thought it was going to be after seeing the trailer, but in a Hollywood littered with trailers that leave absolutely nothing to the imagination, it’s nice to be surprised. This is the directorial debut for Dan Gilroy who is a prominent screenwriter (“Reel Steel” and “The Bourne Legacy”) and he delivers. Bolstered by powerhouse performances, “Nightcrawler” is a creepy thriller that will have you thinking and discussing long after you’ve left the theater.


Genre

“Nightcrawler” is like a lot of its contemporaries in that it doesn’t adhere to one specific genre. A mix of crime, drama and thriller, this quick-paced film delivers a very concise and well told story standing in the shadow of such a brilliant performance that the audience gets swept up in the wants and needs of a sociopath. It is not a big story, don’t expect sweeping twists or huge emotional payoffs, that’s not what Dan Gilroy (who wrote and directed the film) had in mind for this feature. It’s a tight, succinct story about one man and a couple of the choices he makes and how those choices affect the people only around him. There is no world to save, no damsel in distress, no clock to beat. There are car chases and guns and bullet holes and a level of placid violence that is chilling, but the scope remains small and the result is a well delivered film with a message about the current level of acceptance that our society has for gratuitous violence on TV and a performance from Gyllenhaal that is worth seeing in and of itself.

Performance

It’s worth seeing “Nightcrawler” simply for Gyllenhaal. He’s on screen for almost every minute and there is a strong case to be made that the entire film is little more than a character study. And what a character! Gyllenhaal plays Louis Bloom, a man who will stop at nothing to find what he calls, “a career he can grow into”. Gyllenhaal acts with an intensity that is palpable while remaining distant and disconnected from the world around him. With the same intense stare that is so blood-chilling on serial killers, Gyllenhaal talks about everything from wanting a job and being a quick learner, to cooly and calmly blackmailing women into having sex with him. After all of this, after all the inhuman things that we see him do, after he callously films the dead and dying without lifting a finger to help, after all of that, I still cared about him and want him to succeed. It’s here that Gyllenhaal finds the true magic of this performance. We have seen anti-hero’s before, from “Butch Cassidy & The Sundance Kid” to “Ocean’s 11”, the idea of rooting for characters that are on the wrong side of the law is nothing new. In “Nightcrawler” Gyllenhaal and Gilroy take that one step further and ask the audience to care about a man who’s not necessarily a criminal, but could be something much worse. It worked for me, and gave the film a dynamic feeling that helped it pay off come end credits.

Filmmaking

During the opening credits, Gilroy introduces two characters that star in the film and that you won’t be able to find on IMDB - nighttime, and the city of Los Angeles. I talked about Christopher Nolan using space as a character in “Interstellar”, and I saw it again in “Nightcrawler”. Nighttime, in Los Angeles, inside cars - these are characters that are living in their own world. A world where trafficking on human suffering is okay so long as the viewers come in droves. This is the crux of the film, the theme if you will. “Nightcrawler” holds a mirror up to a culture that is obsessed with audience and doesn’t care about trivial things like decency toward human life, respect of privacy or even telling a true story. These people are driven by results and they are going to get them. What better place to grow a character that is willing to succeed at all costs? What better place to see him blossom into a more unique and horrifying type of evil?

What To Watch For

Usually, when given a piece of Hollywood Hunk like Jake Gyllenhaal, the wardrobe department makes him look good enough to eat, whether or not it fits the character on screen. Sex sells and Hollywood knows that, but in this film, Gyllenhaal is on screen wearing baggy button downs that look like hand-me-downs, his hair is greasy and a grimy windbreaker clings to him for the duration of the film. It was because of this wardrobe that when I first saw the trailer, I thought it was a film set in the 1980’s. This small choice informs so much of the character. Here is a man who knows what the corporate “uniform” is, but has no idea that he is not recreating it because he has no sense of “style”. He also doesn’t realize that giving career advice while standing over a dead human being is callous and he will never understand why women dismiss his advances as he talks about sex like a business transaction. This is a man disconnected with the world, and the audience is shown that clearly in the way he presents himself onscreen.
