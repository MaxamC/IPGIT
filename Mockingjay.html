Hunger Games: Mockingjay

First and foremost, it’s important to know that I have not read the books. I bring this up because I’m looking at “Hunger Games: Mockingjay” Part 1 as film number three out of a four-part series. As a film, it is a lot of fun - easily the high point of the series so far. There are a few moments that ask the audience to suspend disbelief (for more, read the “What To Watch For” section) but the filmmaking is breathtaking, the characters are dynamic and the drama is real. One scene reminded me a lot of Richard Attenborough’s “Gandhi” where rebels are running headlong into enemy fire. Scenes like this gave “Mockingjay” a gravitas that had been lacking in the previous films - in this third installment, I finally found myself caring about the characters. While no series will ever come close to the perfection that is “Harry Potter”, this offering by Francis Lawrence does a great job at washing the taste of “Twilight” out of my mouth and giving me a genuine interest in the fourth and final installment.


Genre

This installment of the Hunger Games franchise is easily the most rewarding film of the series. Francis Lawrence is back in the director’s chair (he also directed “Catching Fire”) and is able to create a world that is so much more than a fight between a bunch of teenagers. The huge battle that was the epicenter of the first two films is missing in this one and without it we are left with a movie that is able to tell human stories that I was able to relate to and care about. I would wager that this film will be the best received by lovers of the book because it is able to give time to the nuance and relationships that are often glossed over in order to get to the action. It is these relationships and these moments that draw the audience into the story, which is why this film is easily the best of the series so far. Fans of the first two movies shouldn’t be alarmed, this is still a “Hunger Games" movie, there is no escaping that, but this film focuses more on the people and less on the battle and that makes it a success.

Performance

Jennifer Lawrence is the real deal; every mother-freeking ounce of it. The 24 year old actress already has an Oscar of her own and two more nominations and in “Mockingjay Part 1” she shows exactly why. Surrounded by heavy hitters like Julianne Moore and the late Phillip Seymour Hoffman (who is also astounding, and to whom the film is lovingly dedicated to) she rises above and outshines anyone else on the screen. Other actors come off campy & silly, unable to bring any substance into their “young-adult-novel” characters, but Lawrence hooks the audience and takes us into the head of a young revolutionary in a way that is sure to please book loyalists and movie fans alike. She brings an amount of truth to her roles that is inescapable for the audience. I am constantly left in awe of her talent and the amount of honesty in emotion that she is able to portray on screen again and again; be it a con man’s wife, a dance partner for a mentally unstable man, or a bow-toting revolutionary, Jennifer Lawrence is a wonder to behold.

Filmmaking

Scope. That’s the word that comes to mind as I recall the cinematic qualities of Francis Lawrence’s “Mockingjay”. From the start, establishing shots of the underground bunker that is District 13 are huge, and there is very little obvious CGI to ruin it. From the massiveness of the 13 to the expanse of the devastation of 12, the audience is able to feel the power of situations simply by being shown. The huge scale of the shots lent a seriousness to the uprising, and counteracted the personal nature the dialogue and the character development. Whether it was flying over expansive forests or the sabotaging of a dam, the scope of the film was enormous and incredibly exciting to watch, while the story was able to remain small enough and personal enough so that the audience cared.

What To Watch For

*SPOILER ALERT* The following is going to spoil some of the movie for you. So if you like your movies like you like your milk - keep on walking…reading. No, don’t read! But you know - move it along.

I try not to nitpick. I’ve never made a feature film, I have no idea how hard it would be to get every little thing right. That said, there were three moments in “Mockingjay” where the audience was asked to not only suspend disbelief, but crinkle it up and throw it out the window. The scene in which Jennifer Lawrence goes back to District 12 for the first time, everything is still smoking. Nothing is on fire, it’s presumably been a little bit since the bombs fell, why is there smoke? The shot of the firebombing aftermath is smokeless and harrowing - another example of scope in the filmmaking that I thought was used beautifully. But the smoke when she lands is just so silly. It makes no sense, but it looks good, so that’s why it’s in the movie. Alright, so the whole bombing raid scene made no logical sense to me. From the fact that the bomb shelter, that is forty stories underground is shaking from strikes at the surface, to the premise that they would ever lock Katniss on the other side of those doors, and finally the water. I can hear you asking, what water? In the scene where everyone is stampeding down the stairs to the shelter and then Katniss goes back up to find Prim, there is water pouring down the stairwell. I don’t know why there is so much water pouring into the stairwell, I’m thinking this is much the same as the smoke in district 12, it’s just there to look good. My problem isn’t where it came from, though. My problem is where it goes. When they open the doors to bring Katniss up to the surface to make another video, the steps are banged up, but bone dry. Where did all that water go? I know this detail is asinine and I’m kind of an ass for bringing it up, but I would like an explanation. In my opinion they should have been swimming up that stairwell. This is a small one, and as silly as it may seem, mistakes like this can really take me out of a story. After a field hospital they visit gets attacked by the Capitol, Gale (Liam Hemsworth) and Katniss get separated from their team when a huge smoke stack falls onto the building they’re hiding in. Then Katniss shoots an arrow at an airplane and destroys it and it’s wingman (that’s actually not the unbelievable part). In the next scene, we see that the footage of Katniss shooting the plane down is in the propaganda film. The only problem is that the audience just saw that Katniss and Gale were alone when she shot that arrow - there was a one hundred foot tall big pillar falling through a building to establish this fact - and if that is true, where did they get that footage from? The mystery camera? Again, really cool visual, but it just doesn’t pass the logic test. Are these all silly? Yes? Do they take away from the film? If your mind works like mine does, then yes, for a second they do. But this is a good movie, and little hiccups like this can’t slow it down.
